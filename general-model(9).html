Abstract–Living systems are difficult to reason about without a rigorous idea of what they are. Existing definitions are contentious. This paper constructs a general model of living systems based on two postulates: that they must (1) acquire and (2) retain information. Landauer’s principle and the energy-momentum relation are utilized to apply notions of energy and work in an otherwise abstract context.

Introduction

A living system must do two things: (1) acquire and (2) retain information. Systems that acquire information easily tend to also lose it, while systems that retain information tend not to acquire it. Only living systems do both efficiently[1]^{[1]}[1].

Landauer’s principle states that information cannot be destroyed, only emitted as heat. Therefore any irreversible computation has an energy cost. If the present state of a system does not uniquely determine its past, or if new information from the environment overwrites existing information, energy is lost. The minimum energy required to erase a unit of information is called the Landauer limit
kBT ln2k_BT\ ln2kB​T ln2 where kBk_BkB​ is the Boltzmann constant, TTT is the temperature of the environment, and ln2ln2ln2 is the natural logarithm of 2. In any computation, for every nat of information lost, an amount of energy E≥kBT ln2E \ge k_BT\ ln2E≥kB​T ln2 must be emitted.

The relativistic energy-momentum relation asserts that any particle moving through spacetime has energy
E2=(pc)2+(m0c2)2E^2=(pc)^2+(m_0c^2)^2E2=(pc)2+(m0​c2)2where ppp is momentum and m0m_0m0​ is rest mass. The Landauer limit and energy-momentum relation together permit any quantity of information to be measured in units of momentum, which in turn makes it possible to compute the work required to change a quantity of information.

Discussion

VQ=IQV_Q=I_QVQ​=IQ​ corresponds to information in the environment correlated with internal rotations of the machine, IE transformations that do not collide with the environment.

The model generalizes to machines with memory, including Turing machines, in which case the stable state H(X,Q˘∣Q)=0H(X,\breve Q|Q)=0H(X,Q˘​∣Q)=0 can be disrupted by the evolution of the machine itself as well as by a change in the environment. In general it is impossible to predict whether such a machine will remain in a stable state, for the same reason the halting problem is undecidable.

The concept of aliveness presented here can be applied to systems at any scale. The basic assertion is that aliveness emerges from the overlap between systems that are similar, yet not identical, when knowledge of both yields something contained by neither one individually.
